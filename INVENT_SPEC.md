Evaluate the lighting effectiveness on the cat as subject. Examine how the light in the photograph falls on the cat and determine whether it reveals or obscures the cat's features, texture, and form. Consider whether the cat's face is visible, whether the eyes can be seen, and whether the fur is rendered with enough detail to convey its texture and dimension. Identify problems such as deep shadows that swallow the cat's face or body, harsh overexposure that washes out fur and erases detail, extreme backlighting that reduces the cat to a silhouette, or unnatural color casts that distort the cat's appearance. The standard is not perfect or professional lighting — it is functional lighting. A slightly dim photograph where the cat's features are still discernible scores better than a brightly lit photograph where glare obliterates the cat's face. Score how well the light, whatever its source or character, does its job of making the cat visible, clear, and comfortable to look at. High scores mean the light reveals the cat generously. Low scores mean the light fails to make the cat visible — the subject is lost in shadow, blown out by overexposure, reduced to silhouette by backlighting, or distorted by unnatural color casts.
