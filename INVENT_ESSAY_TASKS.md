1. Assess the visibility of the cat in the photograph. Determine whether the lighting allows the cat to be clearly distinguished from its surroundings. Look for whether the cat's form — its head, ears, body, and overall shape — emerges from the scene or is lost. A cat swallowed by deep shadow, reduced to a dark silhouette against a bright background, or blown out into a featureless white shape by overexposure all fail this assessment. This is not a measure of how bright the image is. A dimly lit photograph where the cat's form is still discernible passes. A brightly lit photograph where glare erases the cat's features fails. The sole question is whether the light, at whatever level it exists, provides enough contrast and clarity for the cat to be perceived as a distinct, recognizable subject in the image.

2. Evaluate how well the lighting reveals the cat's physical detail and texture. Look at whether the light gives dimension to the cat's fur, allowing the viewer to sense its quality — whether it appears soft or coarse, short or long, patterned or solid. Examine whether the cat's eyes catch some light, bringing life and presence to the image. Consider whether the contours of the face, the whiskers, and the shape of the body are rendered with depth rather than flattened into a uniform mass. This task is not about photographic sharpness or resolution. A gently soft image where the lighting sculpts the cat's form and coat with dimension scores well. An image where harsh or flat lighting has stripped away all surface variation and reduced the cat to a featureless shape scores poorly. The question is whether the light does the work of showing what the cat actually looks like — its fur, its features, its physical character.

3. Judge whether the lighting in the photograph feels natural and produces an image that is comfortable to look at. Identify any distortions introduced by the light: unnatural color casts such as a heavy green tint from fluorescent lighting, an overwhelming orange from artificial lamps, or a cold blue from poor white balance. Look for harsh flash artifacts — blank reflective discs in the eyes, hard-edged shadows that carve the face unnaturally, or an unpleasant flatness from direct frontal light. Check for extreme contrast where deep black shadows sit immediately next to blown-out highlights, creating visual discomfort. Naturalness does not require neutral or white light. Warm sunset tones, cool overcast hues, and soft amber lamplight are all acceptable when they feel coherent and honest. What matters is whether the viewer notices the cat before they notice the light — whether the image invites the eye to rest on the subject without being distracted or repelled by how the light has rendered the scene.
